Collecting Like Terms — ELPSA Lesson Plan

This lesson uses Collecting Like Terms as the next visual step after repeated-product work. The key idea is that students do not treat like terms as a simple additive trick; instead they see them as equal groups of the same factor. The circles, drag-and-drop actions and simplification lines help students recognise that the coefficient counts the groups, the variable or repeated factor stays the same, and zero pairs can cancel without changing the overall structure.

Duration: 50–70 min
Focus: multiplicative structure
Tools: Teacher Explore, Student Quiz, Worksheet Creator

Learning intention LI

  • Interpret like terms as equal groups of the same factor.
  • Recognise that collecting like terms means combining the number of groups, not changing the factor.
  • Use the visual model to explain why groups of numbers and groups of pronumerals operate the same way.
  • Use zero pairs and regrouping to simplify expressions efficiently.

Success criteria SC

  • I can explain why 3x + 2x + 5x means 10 groups of x.
  • I can identify what stays the same and what combines when like terms are collected.
  • I can use the circles and drag-and-drop model to explain combining and cancelling.
  • I can connect a numeric example and an algebraic example with the same structure.
Teacher tool shortcuts and flow Keyboard
N new random question   •   R or S simplify   •   Backspace twice to reset
Suggested progression: Number Pattern first for repeated products, then Collecting Like Terms as the next visual bridge into algebra.

Materials & setup Prep

  • Project the Collecting Like Terms teacher tool.
  • Mini whiteboards, markers and erasers.
  • Optional student devices for the Student Quiz.
  • Optional printed fluency from the Worksheet Creator.
Suggested launch examples: 5 groups of 8, 4 groups of x, 7 groups of x, then questions such as 4x + 3x, 6x - 2x, 3ab + 5ab, 8 + 3 + 2, and 4b - 4b.

E Experience — launch with notice and wonder (10–15 min)

Teacher actions Do
  • Display the opening screen with multiple circles, such as groups of 8, groups of x and negative groups of x.
  • Ask students to silently record 2 notices and 1 wonder on mini whiteboards.
  • Invite students to compare a numeric circle and a pronumeral circle: “What is the same? What is different?”
  • Keep the early discussion visual and structural before naming rules.
Notice & wonder prompts Ask
  • “What do you notice about the way each circle is built?”
  • “What stays the same inside one circle?”
  • “What changes from circle to circle?”
  • “How are groups of 8 and groups of x behaving in the same way?”
  • “What might the negative circles represent?”
Teacher talk moves Moves
  • Revoice: “So you’re saying the circle is showing repeated equal groups of the same thing.”
  • Press for structure: “What is the thing being repeated? What is counting the number of repeats?”
  • Compare representations: “Would your reasoning still work if this was x instead of 8?”
  • Hold back the shortcut: avoid starting with “same letters can be added”; keep the language on equal groups.
Bridge statement: “Today we are not learning a shortcut first. We are learning to see like terms as multiple groups of the same factor, and algebra as a continuation of that structure.”

L Language — build meaning and vocabulary (10 min)

Key terms (display + co-construct)
  • Coefficient: the number of groups.
  • Like terms: terms with the same repeated factor or same variable part.
  • Common factor: the part that stays the same.
  • Collecting like terms: combining the number of equal groups.
  • Zero pair: a positive and negative group of the same size that cancel.
  • Equivalent expression: a different-looking expression with the same value.
Mini-whiteboard language practice
  • “The repeated factor is ___.”
  • “The coefficients are ___ and ___.”
  • “This really means ___ groups of ___.”
  • “The collected expression is ___ because ___.”
  • “These two groups make a zero pair because ___.”
Keep cycling between spoken language, circles and symbolic notation.
Teacher moves to strengthen reasoning
  • Link number and algebra: “How is 4 groups of 8 the same structure as 4 groups of x?”
  • Press the invariant: “What part must stay the same for terms to combine?”
  • Name what is added: “Are we adding the x’s, or the number of groups of x?”

P Pictorial — use the circles and drag-and-drop model (15–25 min)

Core routine: “Read the circle”
  1. Identify the factor inside the circle (for example 8, x or ab).
  2. Identify the number of circles or groups.
  3. State the meaning aloud: “___ groups of ___.”
  4. Translate to an expression such as 4x or 5 × 8.
  5. Combine matching circles and explain what changed and what stayed the same.
Teacher explore sequence
  • Begin with single terms and numeric or simple algebraic factors.
  • Use x and a single digit to compare structures side by side.
  • Drag one token from one bubble to another to show combining like terms.
  • Then drag a positive token onto a matching negative token to show a zero pair.
  • Move later to double terms such as ab, 7b or 3x.
What students should notice
  • The inside factor must match for circles to combine.
  • The number of groups changes, but the repeated factor stays the same.
  • Positive and negative groups of the same factor can cancel as zero pairs.
  • The model is deliberately capped at 10 items per circle to keep the visual manageable and maintain clear multiplicative structure.
Teacher note: make the 10-item limit explicit. Explain that the limit is there to reduce cognitive load and keep the model readable; when totals grow larger, students can use place value partitioning / splitting to reason multiplicatively beyond the visual cap.
Mini-whiteboard prompts during pictorial
  • “What is the value of this circle?”
  • “What would this become after one drag?”
  • “Which circles can combine? Why?”
  • “Which circles make a zero pair?”
  • “How many groups would remain after the cancellation?”
Common misconceptions to surface
  • Thinking any two terms can combine, even when the factor differs.
  • Treating the letters as the part being added, rather than the number of groups.
  • Ignoring the role of negative terms in cancellation.
  • Seeing the model as counting objects only, rather than representing multiplication.
Teacher move: “Point to the part that must stay the same. Point to the part that counts the groups.”

S Symbolic — generalise the rule (10–20 min)

Teacher-led modelling (I do / we do)
  • Model a numeric structure first, such as 3 × 8 + 2 × 8.
  • Rewrite as (3 + 2) × 8, then simplify to 5 × 8.
  • Connect this to the algebraic version 3x + 2x = 5x.
  • Say explicitly: “We are not adding x and x as separate labels. We are adding the number of groups of x.”
Student symbolic practice (you do)
  • Write the collected form of a generated expression.
  • Explain in words what was added first.
  • State the common factor and the total coefficient.
  • Compare a long calculation method with a collected method.
  • Explain why an unlike-term pair such as 3x + 2y cannot combine.
Suggested progression from numeric to algebraic
  • 4 × 9 + 3 × 9 → 7 × 9
  • 5 × 38 + 2 × 38 → 7 × 38
  • 4x + 3x → 7x
  • 6ab + 5ab → 11ab
  • 7x - 2x → 5x
This sequence keeps the structure constant while changing the surface features.

A Application — practice + transfer (15–30+ min)

Practice options using your tools
  • Student Quiz: differentiated independent practice.
  • Worksheet Creator: printed fluency and consolidation.
How the Student Quiz can be framed: students solve expressions by identifying the repeated factor, combining the coefficients, and checking whether any cancelling is required. Teachers can direct students toward the more supported mode first, then remove visual support as confidence improves.
Suggested success path: supported visual practice first, then symbolic-only fluency.
Transfer and extension ideas
  • Real-world grouping: connect to packs, rows, teams or arrays where the repeated unit is fixed and the number of groups changes.
  • Open middle: create different expressions that all simplify to the same target, such as 12x.
  • Generalise: explain why 4ab + 7ab works but 4ab + 7a does not.
  • Structure comparison: compare efficient collection with full expansion-style calculation.
Challenge tasks
  • Make three different expressions that all simplify to 12x.
  • Create one numeric example and one algebraic example with exactly the same structure.
  • Write an expression with 5 terms that simplifies to 11ab.
  • Explain why 3x + 2y cannot be collected in the same way.

Assessment for learning AFL

Checks during the lesson
  • Mini-whiteboard “show me” responses after each variation.
  • Ask students to identify the common factor and total coefficient.
  • Listen for explanations using “groups of” language.
  • Check whether students can move from numeric to algebraic examples without changing their reasoning.
Exit ticket (2 minutes)
  • Simplify 4 × 9 + 3 × 9 + 2 × 9 and explain why your method is efficient.
  • Simplify 5x + 6x.
  • Write one sentence explaining what collecting like terms really means.

Differentiation Support / Extend

Support
  • Keep the repeated factor numeric first before moving to x or ab.
  • Use smaller totals such as 5, 6, 7 or 8.
  • Leave visual support on and discuss each drag move aloud.
  • Use 2 or 3 terms before increasing complexity.
Extension
  • Use larger totals and more terms.
  • Turn on algebraic examples with two-letter factors such as ab.
  • Ask students to compare collected structure with full expansion-style calculation.
  • Require students to create their own expression to match a target simplified form.

Teacher notes Notes

  • Keep the focus on structure, not answer-getting.
  • Return often to the sentence frame: “This is ___ groups of ___.”
  • Use the drag-and-drop and zero-pair actions as conceptual teaching moves, not just interactive features.
  • When totals exceed the visual model, connect back to place value splitting / partitioning so students still think multiplicatively.
  • Avoid reducing the lesson too early to “same letters can be added”; the goal is for algebra to feel sensible and connected to prior multiplication structure.
